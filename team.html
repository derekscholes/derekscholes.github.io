Derek Scholes, MD, RDN, MDA

Personal Statement

As a registered dietitian, I believe that food as medicine is essential—but I also noted that it falls short. During my dietetic internship, I encountered patients whose acute or chronic diseases could not be managed by nutrition alone, revealing the limits of dietary therapy in complex pathology. These experiences shaped my approach to patient care through a dual lens that views disease as inseparable from nutrition, metabolism, and long-term health, while recognizing medicine’s role when nutrition alone falls short. This perspective became the foundation of my commitment to gastroenterology and advancing equitable care.

While pursuing dietetic registration, I had early exposure to gastroenterology while shadowing at a surgical center. I was drawn to this field as it integrates procedural interventions with nutritional and medical prevention, as well as long-term management. Gastroenterology uniquely allows me to leverage my educational background to combine metabolic, nutritional, and procedural approaches to alter disease trajectories and improve outcomes for diverse populations.

Much of my research focused on clinical outcomes and health disparities across gastroenterology and hepatology. I have contributed to study design, data analysis, and national presentations that are both professionally meaningful and personally connected to my Hispanic background. These experiences strengthened my commitment to research that informs and creates equitable care.

My most formative clinical experience occurred during my transplant hepatology rotation. I cared for a Spanish-speaking woman with drug-induced liver injury who progressed to acute liver failure. While language concordance allowed me to build trust and ensure she understood her care, her greatest barrier was systemic. She obtained out-of-state Medicaid while inpatient, which was not accepted at our transplant center. At a critical moment, her access to life-saving care was limited not by medicine, but by policy. I worked with the transplant attending to coordinate her transfer to a center that could evaluate her for transplantation. This experience taught me to anticipate barriers, navigate complex systems, and advocate decisively to ensure timely access to care. It reinforced the importance of multidisciplinary coordination, cultural humility, and physician leadership in high-stakes situations.

Systems-based care is another interest that I further developed through coordinating the malnutrition quality improvement initiative as part of my Master’s in Dietetics Administration during my 4th year of medical school. This was driven in collaboration with the clinical nutrition department at Howard University to improve the diagnosis and treatment of moderate and severe malnutrition. I led multidisciplinary collaboration across administration, nursing, and clinical services. This project helped me recognize the widespread and underappreciated impact of malnutrition across patient populations and how to address it to improve patient outcomes across multiple specialty services.

My purpose is grounded in my dual training in medicine and nutrition, and shaped by experiences that have highlighted both clinical complexity and structural barriers to care. I aspire to become a gastroenterologist who delivers high-quality, patient-centered care while advancing equitable solutions through quality improvement and advocacy. By integrating nutrition, evidence-based medicine, and health equity, I hope to improve outcomes for diverse and underserved populations through either rural private practice or therapeutic endoscopy.
